Paraphrased Research Notes:

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy article on Philosophy of Religion provides a comprehensive overview of the field. For the purpose of generating syllogisms, the most relevant sections are those dealing with arguments for and against the existence of God.

**1. Ontological Arguments (Section 5.2.1)**

*   **Core Idea:** These arguments are a priori, based on the concept of God as a maximally perfect being.
*   **Anselm's Formulation:** God is defined as a being than which nothing greater can be conceived. Since it is greater to exist in reality than only in the mind, God must exist in reality.
*   **Modern Modal Version:**
    *   Premise 1: It is possible that a maximally great being exists.
    *   Premise 2: If it is possible that a maximally great being exists, then a maximally great being exists in some possible world.
    *   Premise 3: If a maximally great being exists in some possible world, then it exists in every possible world (because necessary existence is a great-making property).
    *   Premise 4: If a maximally great being exists in every possible world, then it exists in the actual world.
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, a maximally great being exists in the actual world.
*   **Criticisms (Gaunilo, Kant):**
    *   Gaunilo's "Lost Island": One can conceive of a perfect island, but that doesn't mean it exists. (Reply: The concept of a "perfect island" is not coherent in the same way as a "maximally great being").
    *   Kant's "Existence is not a predicate": Existence is not a property that adds to the concept of a thing. To say something exists is to say the concept is instantiated in the world.

**2. Cosmological Arguments (Section 5.2.2)**

*   **Core Idea:** These arguments are a posteriori, moving from the existence of the cosmos to the existence of a First Cause or a necessary being.
*   **Argument from Contingency (Aquinas, Leibniz):**
    *   Premise 1: Everything that exists has a reason for its existence (Principle of Sufficient Reason).
    *   Premise 2: The universe is contingent; it could have not existed.
    *   Premise 3: The reason for the existence of the universe must be either other contingent things or a necessary being.
    *   Premise 4: An infinite regress of contingent things cannot provide a sufficient reason for the existence of the whole chain.
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, a necessary being, which is the reason for its own existence, must exist. This being is God.
*   **Kalam Cosmological Argument:**
    *   Premise 1: Whatever begins to exist has a cause.
    *   Premise 2: The universe began to exist (supported by Big Bang cosmology).
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, the universe has a cause. This cause must be personal, timeless, and immensely powerful.
*   **Criticisms (Hume, Russell):**
    *   The universe as a whole may not need an explanation, even if its parts do (fallacy of composition).
    *   Why assume the Principle of Sufficient Reason is true?
    *   The argument doesn't necessarily lead to the God of theism, just a powerful first cause.

**3. Teleological Arguments (Design Arguments) (Section 5.2.3)**

*   **Core Idea:** The order, complexity, and apparent purpose in the universe point to an intelligent designer.
*   **Argument from Analogy (Paley's Watchmaker):**
    *   Premise 1: Human artifacts (like a watch) are products of intelligent design.
    *   Premise 2: The universe resembles these human artifacts in its complexity and order, but on a much grander scale.
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, the universe is likely the product of a much more powerful and intelligent designer (God).
*   **Fine-Tuning Argument:**
    *   Premise 1: The fundamental physical constants of the universe (e.g., gravity, the cosmological constant) are "fine-tuned" within an extremely narrow range to permit life.
    *   Premise 2: This fine-tuning is due to either physical necessity, chance, or design.
    *   Premise 3: It is not due to physical necessity or chance (the probability is too low).
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, the fine-tuning is due to design.
*   **Criticisms (Hume, Darwin):**
    *   The analogy between the universe and human artifacts is weak. The universe is a unique case.
    *   Evolution by natural selection provides a powerful alternative explanation for biological complexity without recourse to a designer.
    *   The multiverse hypothesis could explain fine-tuning by chance (if there are infinite universes, one like ours is bound to exist).

**4. The Problem of Evil (Argument against God's Existence) (Section 5.2.4)**

*   **Core Idea:** The existence of evil (both moral and natural) is incompatible with or evidence against the existence of an omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good God.
*   **Logical Problem of Evil:**
    *   Premise 1: If God exists, God is omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good.
    *   Premise 2: An omnipotent being can prevent any evil.
    *   Premise 3: An omniscient being knows about all evil.
    *   Premise 4: A perfectly good being would want to prevent all evil.
    *   Premise 5: Evil exists.
    *   Conclusion: Therefore, a God who is omnipotent, omniscient, and perfectly good does not exist.
*   **Evidential Problem of Evil:** The sheer quantity and gratuitous nature of evil in the world make it highly improbable that God exists.
*   **Theodicies (Responses):**
    *   **Free Will Defense:** God gave humans free will, which is a great good. Evil is a necessary consequence of the misuse of free will. This cannot be avoided without removing the greater good of freedom.
    *   **Soul-Making Theodicy (John Hick):** The world is a place of challenge and adversity, designed to build character and allow for spiritual growth. Evil is necessary for this "soul-making" process.
    *   **Skeptical Theism:** We are not in a position to know God's reasons for allowing evil. Our limited understanding cannot be a basis for concluding that there are no good reasons.
